Business Dispute: Should You Mediate or Go to Court?

Commercial · ~5 min read

When a client won't pay, a contract falls apart, or a partnership turns sour, the instinct is to "lawyer up and sue." But for most commercial disputes, mediation gets you a faster, cheaper outcome — and often saves the relationship too. Here's how the two compare.

1. Time: weeks vs months (or years)

Litigation moves through filing, discovery, hearings and judgment. It can take many months — sometimes years. Mediation is scheduled around the parties and most matters settle within one to four weeks. When cash flow is on the line, speed is money.

2. Cost: predictable vs open-ended

Court means filing fees, legal fees and your own time — and the bill is hard to predict. Mediation is typically a fixed, quoted fee, with far lower total cost.

3. The relationship: preserved vs broken

Suing a client, supplier or partner usually ends the relationship. Mediation focuses on a workable solution, so you can often keep doing business afterwards.

💡 A mediator is a neutral third party. They don't take sides — they help both parties get to a deal each can live with and sign.

4. Privacy: confidential vs public

Court hearings and judgments can be public. Mediation is private, protecting your commercial information and reputation.

When does mediation make sense?

  • Both sides can still communicate, even if positions are far apart;
  • You want a fast, low-cost resolution;
  • The ongoing relationship has value;
  • You'd rather keep the matter out of the public record.

Worried they'll agree and then walk away?

A signed mediation agreement is a binding contract, and it can be made enforceable through the appropriate legal channels — so a party who breaches it can be held to it. A good mediator builds that protection into the agreement from the start.
