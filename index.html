FAQs

Questions people ask about mediation

How is mediation different from going to court?

The biggest difference is control: in mediation, both parties shape the outcome; in court, a judge decides for you.

  • Time — most mediations finish in 1–4 weeks; litigation often takes a year or more
  • Cost — a predictable fixed fee vs. escalating court and legal costs
  • Privacy — fully confidential vs. public hearings and judgments
  • Relationships — solve the problem while keeping the relationship workable

And you give up nothing by trying — if mediation doesn't resolve it, every legal option remains open.

Is a mediated agreement legally binding?

The honest answer: a mediated agreement is not automatically enforceable in most cases — and that's actually why mediation works, not a weakness.

Because the outcome is one both parties negotiated and see as being in their own interest, compliance rates are high — people keep agreements they chose, far more readily than orders imposed on them.

It's also worth being realistic: even a binding agreement or court judgment can be slow, costly and difficult to enforce in practice. A workable deal both sides actually want to honour beats a "binding" one on paper. Where extra security matters, the agreement can be formalised (for example as a deed or consent orders).

How long does it take?

Most matters resolve within 1–4 weeks of first contact.

  • Straightforward debt or neighbour matters — often settled in a single 2–3 hour session
  • Typical matters — one or two sessions
  • Complex, multi-party or high-value matters — several sessions

Still dramatically faster than litigation, which routinely runs for months or years.

How much does it cost?

Your first consultation is free — we assess whether mediation suits your situation before you commit to anything.

  • A clear fixed quote before any mediation begins — no hidden extras
  • Standard matters are charged per session or per matter
  • Fees are typically shared between the parties (other arrangements can be agreed)

Overall, mediation usually costs a small fraction of what litigation would.

What if the other party won't take part?

This is the most common worry — and it's usually solvable.

We contact the other party as a neutral third party, explaining the confidentiality, low cost and benefits for both sides. Many people initially refuse simply because they don't know how mediation works, or fear that agreeing looks weak — an invitation from a professional mediator lands very differently from one made by the other side.

Mediation is voluntary; if they still decline, we'll tell you honestly and talk through your remaining options.

Do I need a lawyer?

You don't need a lawyer at the mediation table — the process is designed to be accessible, and most participants represent themselves.

We do strongly recommend getting legal advice before mediation begins, so you understand your position, your bottom line, and what litigation would really cost you. Parties who arrive well-informed negotiate with confidence — and are significantly more likely to reach an agreement they won't regret.

The mediator is strictly neutral and doesn't give legal advice to either side.

Is everything confidential?

Yes — and this is one of mediation's biggest advantages over a public courtroom.

  • Sessions are private, not public hearings
  • Offers, concessions and information disclosed won't be shared without your consent
  • As a general rule, what's said in mediation can't later be used as evidence in court

That protection is what lets both sides speak openly — which is exactly what gets disputes resolved.

Can we mediate online?

Absolutely. Video mediation is available for every service we offer and works just as well as meeting in person.

It's ideal when the parties are in different cities or countries, or simply prefer not to be in the same room. We handle scheduling and setup, agreements can be signed electronically, and hybrid arrangements (one party online, one in person) work too.
